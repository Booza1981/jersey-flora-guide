Hello Nature Explorer! A Guide to Jersey's Amazing Trees and Flowers

(Section 1) Welcome, Little Explorer!

Hello! Are you ready for an adventure? Jersey is full of amazing plants! Tall trees reach for the sky, and colourful wildflowers hide like jewels in the grass and hedges. Let's go exploring together and see what we can find!

This guide is like a treasure map to help us become super Plant Detectives. We can look closely at the shapes of the leaves, feel the bumpy bark on the trees, see all the pretty colours of the flowers, and maybe even find some seeds or berries!

There's a special checklist at the end so we can tick off all the wonderful trees and flowers we spot on our adventure. Grab your grown-up, put on your explorer hat, and let's go find some plants!

(Section 2) Wonderful Trees of Jersey

Trees are amazing big plants. They have strong, woody trunks and lots of branches that reach up high, like arms waving hello. Their leaves help them make food from sunshine! Let's meet some of the friendly trees that live here in Jersey. Many organisations, like Jersey Trees for Life, work hard to plant and look after trees because they are so important for our island.1

(Subsection 2.1) Tree Spotter Pages

(Page 1: English Oak)

English Oak (Quercus robur)

"I Look Like This!"

I am a big, strong tree! People have thought oaks are symbols of strength for a very long time.3 I can live for hundreds of years.5

"My Leaves"

My leaves have special wiggly edges, like puzzle pieces! They turn brown in autumn.3

"My Bark"

Feel my bumpy, wrinkly bark! Its quite rough.3

"My Flowers"

In spring, look for my dangly, stringy flowers called catkins.4

"My Seeds"

Can you find my little nutty seeds on the ground? They are called acorns and wear little hats called cups!.3

"Wow! Fact"

Squirrels, mice, and badgers love to hide and eat my acorns! Birds also love to visit my branches.3

(Page 2: Silver Birch)

Silver Birch (Betula pendula)

"I Look Like This!"

I am a graceful tree, often looking tall and slim. I'm known as a 'pioneer' because I can grow in lots of different places, even where the soil isn't very rich.9

"My Leaves"

My leaves are quite small and shaped like little triangles with jagged edges. They turn bright yellow in autumn.9

"My Bark"

My bark is my most special feature it's bright white or silvery! Sometimes it peels off like thin paper.9 Younger trees might have darker bark first.9

"My Flowers"

Like the Oak, I have dangly flowers called catkins. They release tiny seeds that the wind carries far away.9

"My Seeds"

My seeds are packed inside the catkins and are super tiny!.9

"Wow! Fact"

Lots of tiny insects and caterpillars love to munch on my leaves, which makes me a great snack bar for birds!.9

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 3: Common Hawthorn)

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna)

"I Look Like This!"

I often grow in hedges and can be quite bushy and dense. Watch out I have sharp thorns!.13 I can grow into a small tree too, reaching up to 15 metres.13

"My Leaves"

My leaves are small, usually with 3 to 7 pointy bits called lobes.15 They are often one of the first green leaves you'll see in spring.13

"My Bark"

My bark is brown-grey and gets quite knotted and cracked as I get older.13 My twigs are thin, brown, and covered in sharp thorns.13

"My Flowers"

In May, I'm covered in pretty, sweet-smelling white or pink flowers! That's why some people call me the 'May Tree'.13

"My Seeds"

Look for my bright red berries in autumn! They are called 'haws'.13

"Wow! Fact"

Birds like blackbirds and thrushes love to eat my red haws in the winter! My thorny branches also make a safe place for birds to build nests.13

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 4: Common Hazel)

Common Hazel (Corylus avellana)

"I Look Like This!"

I usually grow as a bushy shrub with lots of stems, but sometimes I grow into a small tree.18 I'm often found in woods and hedges.20

"My Leaves"

My leaves are quite round, a bit fuzzy (especially underneath), and have a pointy tip.20 They feel soft to touch.20

"My Bark"

My bark is smooth and greyish-brown, sometimes shiny, and it might peel a bit when I get older.20

"My Flowers"

In late winter, before my leaves appear, look for my long, dangly yellow flowers called catkins. They look like little lambs' tails!.18 My tiny red female flowers are harder to spot.21

"My Seeds"

Yum! I grow delicious nuts called hazelnuts (or cobnuts). You'll find them hiding inside leafy little cups.18

"Wow! Fact"

Squirrels and dormice absolutely love my tasty hazelnuts! They gather them in autumn to store for winter.18

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 5: Rowan / Mountain Ash)

Rowan / Mountain Ash (Sorbus aucuparia)

"I Look Like This!"

I am a slender, graceful tree. Even though I'm sometimes called 'Mountain Ash', I'm not actually an Ash tree!.23 I can grow quite high up on hills.23

"My Leaves"

My leaves look like feathers! Each leaf is made up of lots of smaller leaflets (usually 9-15) arranged in pairs along a stem.24 They turn lovely red colours in autumn.23

"My Bark"

My bark is smooth and silvery-grey, especially when I'm young.23

"My Flowers"

In late spring, I have big, flat clusters of small, creamy-white flowers. They have a strong scent!.23

"My Seeds"

My most famous feature! In autumn, I'm covered in bunches of bright, shiny red berries.23

"Wow! Fact"

Birds, especially thrushes like redwings and fieldfares arriving for winter, think my berries are a super tasty treat!.23

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 6: Elder)

Elder (Sambucus nigra)

"I Look Like This!"

I often grow as a large shrub or small tree, sometimes near rabbit homes!.28 I don't usually have lots and lots of branches.28

"My Leaves"

My leaves are made up of 5 to 7 smaller oval leaflets with slightly jagged edges.28 If you crush them, they have a strong, unusual smell!.28

"My Bark"

My bark is grey-brown and feels quite rough and corky, with ridges or furrows, especially when I'm older.28 My twigs might have spongy stuff inside.28

"My Flowers"

In early summer, look for my huge, flat bunches of tiny, creamy-white flowers. They smell very sweet!.28

"My Seeds"

After the flowers, I grow lots of small, juicy, dark purple or black berries hanging in bunches.28

"Wow! Fact"

Lots of insects love the nectar in my flowers, and birds and little mammals like dormice enjoy eating my berries!.28

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 7: Field Maple)

Field Maple (Acer campestre)

"I Look Like This!"

I am the only maple tree that is truly native to the UK!.32 I often grow in hedges and woods and don't usually get as big as trees like Oak or Beech.32 I can live for a very long time, maybe 350 years!.32

"My Leaves"

My leaves have five rounded lobes, a bit like a hand shape, but smaller than Sycamore leaves.32 They turn a beautiful golden yellow in autumn.34

"My Bark"

My bark is light brown and can become flaky or corky with ridges as I get older. Even my twigs can get corky!.32

"My Flowers"

My flowers are small and yellowy-green, hanging in little bunches. They appear around the same time as my leaves.32

"My Seeds"

Whee! My seeds have wings! They grow in pairs and spin down like little helicopters when the wind blows.32 The wings stick out straight, not angled like Sycamore seeds.33

"Wow! Fact"

Tiny insects called aphids like my leaves, which means ladybirds and birds come to eat the aphids! Bees like my flowers too.32

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 8: European Beech)

European Beech (Fagus sylvatica)

"I Look Like This!"

I am a tall, grand tree with a wide, spreading shape, sometimes called the 'Queen of the Woods'.37 I can grow up to 40 metres tall!.39

"My Leaves"

My leaves are oval shaped with slightly wavy edges and pointy tips. They are shiny green in summer and turn golden brown in autumn.37 Sometimes my leaves stay on the branches all winter!

"My Bark"

My bark is very smooth and grey, like an elephant's skin! Sometimes it has little horizontal marks on it.37

"My Flowers"

In spring, I have dangly, tassel-like male flowers and small female flowers that grow in pairs inside a little cup.37

"My Seeds"

My seeds are nuts called 'beechmast'. They grow inside prickly brown cases that split open in autumn. There are usually two or three nuts inside each case.37

"Wow! Fact"

Squirrels, mice, voles, and birds love to eat my tasty nuts! In the old days, farmers used to let pigs eat them to get fat.37

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 9: Holly)

Holly (Ilex aquifolium)

"I Look Like This!"

I am famous for my shiny, prickly leaves and bright red berries, especially at Christmas time! I stay green all year round.41 I can grow as a bush or a tree up to 15 metres tall.42

"My Leaves"

My leaves are dark green, shiny, and leathery. Most people know me for my sharp spikes! But sometimes, higher up on older trees, my leaves can be smooth.42

"My Bark"

My bark is smooth and grey or brownish, often with little bumps that look like warts.42

"My Flowers"

In spring or early summer, I have small white flowers with four petals. Some trees have boy flowers and some have girl flowers.42

"My Seeds"

Only the girl trees grow berries! My berries are bright, shiny red and stay on the tree through winter.42 (Sometimes berries can be yellow or black, but red is most common).41

"Wow! Fact"

Birds love to eat my berries in winter when other food is hard to find! My dense, prickly leaves also make a super safe place for birds to hide and for hedgehogs to sleep.41

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 10: Goat Willow / Pussy Willow)

Goat Willow / Pussy Willow (Salix caprea)

"I Look Like This!"

I am a type of willow, but my leaves are wider than most other willows. I can grow as a shrub or a small tree, up to about 10 metres tall.45

"My Leaves"

My leaves are oval-shaped, not long and thin like many willows. They are green on top and grey and fuzzy underneath, with a tip that often bends to one side.45

"My Bark"

My bark is grey-brown and quite smooth when I'm young, but it gets cracks that look like diamond shapes as I get older.45

"My Flowers"

My flowers are famous! In early spring, before my leaves come out, I grow fluffy catkins. The boy catkins are grey and fuzzy at first (like a cat's paw or 'pussy willow'!), then turn yellow with pollen. The girl catkins are green.45

"My Seeds"

After the bees and the wind have visited my flowers, the girl catkins grow into fluffy seeds that float away on the breeze.45

"Wow! Fact"

My fluffy catkins are one of the very first foods for bees waking up in early spring! My leaves are also super important food for the caterpillars of beautiful butterflies, like the amazing Purple Emperor!.45

(Section 3) Wonderful Wildflowers of Jersey

Wildflowers are like colourful jewels hiding in nature! You can find them sparkling in grassy meadows 48, clinging bravely to cliffs by the sea 50, peeking out from hedgerows, and brightening up shady woods.52 Different flowers like different homes. Let's see which ones we can find on our Jersey adventure!

(Subsection 3.1) Wildflower Spotter Pages

(Page 11: Red Campion)

Red Campion (Silene dioica)

"My Flower Power!"

My flowers are a cheerful bright pink or rose-red! Each flower has five petals that look like they have a little notch cut out at the end.54

"My Leaves"

My leaves grow in pairs opposite each other on the stem. They feel soft and fuzzy because they are covered in tiny hairs.54

"See Me Grow!"

Find me brightening up shady woods, path edges, and hedgerows, especially after the bluebells have finished flowering.54 I can grow up to 1 metre tall.54

"Wow! Fact"

Butterflies and long-tongued bumblebees love to visit me for a drink of sweet nectar!.54 Some people say fairies hide in my flowers!.55

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 12: Foxglove)

Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea)

"My Flower Power!"

I have tall spikes covered in beautiful bell-shaped flowers! They are usually pinky-purple, but sometimes white, and often have spots inside like little freckles.58

"My Leaves"

My leaves are large, oval-shaped, and feel quite hairy. They grow in a clump close to the ground in my first year.58

"See Me Grow!"

Look for my tall flower spikes (they can be up to 2 metres high!) along woodland edges, hedges, and sometimes in gardens.58

"Wow! Fact"

Bumblebees love to crawl right inside my bell-shaped flowers to get the nectar!.58

!! Grown-Ups: Look, Don't Touch! All parts of Foxglove are poisonous if eaten.!! 58

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 13: Sea Thrift)

Sea Thrift (Armeria maritima)

"My Flower Power!"

My flowers look like little pink pom-poms sitting on top of long, thin stalks! Sometimes my flowers are white or lavender.63

"My Leaves"

My leaves are long, thin, and green, like blades of grass. They grow close together in a neat cushion shape near the ground.63

"See Me Grow!"

As my name suggests, I love living near the sea! Find me growing on cliffs, sand dunes, and rocky places where it might be salty.63 I don't mind the sea spray!.66

"Wow! Fact"

Bees and butterflies love visiting my bright pink flower heads for a tasty nectar snack!.65

(Page 14: Common Gorse)

Common Gorse (Ulex europaeus)

"My Flower Power!"

My flowers are bright, sunshine yellow and look a bit like pea flowers! They have a lovely smell, a bit like coconuts.69 I can have flowers almost all year round!.69

"My Leaves"

Ouch! Be careful! My leaves aren't soft they are sharp, pointy green spikes or needles!.69

"See Me Grow!"

I grow as a big, dense, spiky bush. You'll often find me covering hillsides, heathland, and cliffs, especially near the coast.69

"Wow! Fact"

My dense, prickly branches make a super safe home for birds like the Stonechat and Dartford Warbler to hide and build their nests!.69 In hot weather, my seed pods pop open with a crackling sound!.73

(Page 15: Bluebell)

Bluebell (Hyacinthoides non-scripta)

"My Flower Power!"

I have beautiful bell-shaped flowers that are usually a lovely deep blue-violet colour. They hang downwards, mostly on one side of the stem, and have a sweet smell.53 Look inside for creamy-white pollen.53

"My Leaves"

My leaves are long, narrow, and strap-shaped, growing from the base of the plant.74

"See Me Grow!"

In spring (April-May), I love to grow all together, making amazing blue carpets in woods! Here in Jersey, you can also find me on cliffs and in hedgerows.53

"Wow! Fact"

Butterflies like the Brimstone and bees enjoy sipping nectar from my sweet-smelling flowers!.74

Spotted it? [ ]

(Page 16: Buttercup)

Buttercup (Ranunculus species)

"My Flower Power!"

My flowers are like little cups of sunshine! They are bright yellow and look shiny, almost like they're made of butter.77 They usually have five petals.79

"My Leaves"

My leaves are green and usually divided into three or more parts, sometimes looking a bit like parsley.78

"See Me Grow!"

I love growing in grassy places like meadows, fields, and sometimes even lawns!.78

"Wow! Fact"

Ask a grown-up to hold my shiny flower under your chin. Does it make your chin look yellow? People say that means you like butter!.77 Bees and other insects like visiting my flowers too.80

!! Grown-Ups: Can cause mild skin irritation. Best not to eat.!! 77

(Page 17: Primrose)

Primrose (Primula vulgaris)

"My Flower Power!"

I am one of the first flowers to say hello in spring! My flowers are usually a soft, pale yellow colour, sometimes almost creamy-white. Each flower has five petals that look like they have a little notch at the end.76

"My Leaves"

My leaves are green and look quite wrinkly. They grow in a circle close to the ground, like a rosette.76

"See Me Grow!"

Look for me in shady places like woods, under hedges, and in grassy banks, especially in early spring.76

"Wow! Fact"

Butterflies that wake up early in spring, like the Brimstone, are happy to find my flowers for a nectar drink!.76 Long ago, people thought I could protect homes from fairies!.76

(Page 18: Jersey Orchid)

Jersey Orchid (Anacamptis laxiflora)

"My Flower Power!"

I am a very special flower for Jersey! My flowers are usually a beautiful rich pinkish-purple colour, though sometimes they can be paler pink or even white.48 They grow spaced out along my tall stem.48

"My Leaves"

My leaves are long and narrow, growing up my stem.48

"See Me Grow!"

You need to look for me in special damp, grassy fields, like the amazing orchid fields at Le Noir Pr.48 I flower from May to July.48

"Wow! Fact"

Like other orchids, I have tiny, tiny seeds, like dust!.85 Bees and other insects help to pollinate my flowers.87

(Page 19: Pyramidal Orchid)

Pyramidal Orchid (Anacamptis pyramidalis)

"My Flower Power!"

Can you see why I have my name? My bright pink or purple flowers grow packed tightly together in a shape like a pyramid or a cone!.48

"My Leaves"

My leaves are long, thin, and pointed, growing up my stem.89

"See Me Grow!"

Find me in sunny, grassy places, often where the soil is chalky, and sometimes near the coast on sand dunes.48 I flower in June and July.89

"Wow! Fact"

Butterflies and moths especially love my flowers! They use their long tongues (proboscis) to reach the nectar hidden inside.89

(Section 5) Keep Exploring! More Jersey Nature Fun

Did you enjoy being a Plant Detective? There's always more to discover about Jersey's wonderful nature! Here are some ideas for grown-ups to help you keep exploring:

●     Look for Local Books: Ask at a local bookshop or library for guides like "The Wild Flowers of Jersey" 92 or "Channel Island Flora".94 These might show even more plants you can find. There's also an online booklet from the government about Jersey's very rare and protected plants, showing why it's important not to pick flowers.96

●     Visit Local Nature Organisations Online: These groups care for Jersey's nature and sometimes have events or information for families:

○     Jersey Trees for Life: They know all about trees! Maybe they have fun tree events?.1

○     The National Trust for Jersey: They look after special places, including fields full of Jersey Orchids (Le Noir Pr) and other beautiful natural areas where you can explore.48 Check their website for family events or nature walks.98

○     Socit Jersiaise: They have experts who study plants (a Botany section) and might have talks, walks, or more books about Jersey's nature.93

○     Durrell Wildlife Conservation Trust: While famous for animals, they might have activities or information linking animals and local plants at Jersey Zoo.100

○     Jersey Biodiversity Centre: You can tell them about the plants you see and find out more about local wildlife.53

●     Explore UK Nature Websites: Websites from big UK nature groups often have great ideas for exploring nature, printable spotter sheets, and craft activities that you can do anywhere:

○     Wildlife Watch (The Wildlife Trusts): They have loads of fun activities and spotter sheets you can download.102

○     Woodland Trust: They have 'Tree Tools for Schools' with spotter sheets for leaves, twigs, and seeds that are great for any woodland walk.104

Keep your eyes open every time you go outside nature is always changing!

(Section 6) A Note for Grown-Ups

Exploring nature with your daughter sounds like a wonderful way to connect and learn together! This guide is designed to be a simple starting point. Here are a few important things to keep in mind:

●     Safety First - Look, Don't Lick! It's vital to teach children never to eat any part of a wild plant unless you are 100% certain it is safe. Some common plants included here are poisonous if ingested, particularly Foxglove 58 but also potentially Buttercups (can cause skin irritation and are poisonous) 77 and raw Elder berries.30 Other plants not included could also be harmful. The safest rule is simply to admire plants with our eyes and hands (gently!), but never our mouths. Washing hands after exploring is always a good idea too.

●     Look, Don't Pick - Leave Wildflowers for Everyone: Please encourage admiring wildflowers where they grow. Picking them means others can't enjoy their beauty, and crucially, it prevents the plant from making seeds for next year. It also removes a food source for pollinators like bees and butterflies. Furthermore, some wildflowers in Jersey are legally protected because they are very rare.96 The best approach is to leave all wildflowers untouched for wildlife and other people to appreciate.

●     Native Plants are Superstars: You might hear about 'native' plants. These are plants that have grown naturally in Jersey for a very, very long time, like our native English Bluebell 53 or the special Jersey Orchid.48 Local wildlife, like insects and birds, are perfectly adapted to using these native plants for food and shelter, making them incredibly important for Jersey's biodiversity.106 Some other plants might have been brought here from other countries (like the Spanish Bluebell, which can cause problems for our native one 53). Choosing native plants for gardens is a great way to help local wildlife! This aligns with broader conservation efforts in Jersey, such as the Pollinator Project.108

●     Keep the Adventure Going: Nature changes with the seasons. Revisit favourite spots to see how plants change from buds to flowers to fruits and seeds. Use the checklist multiple times! The simple act of observing and asking questions ("What colour is that flower?", "How does that bark feel?") fosters curiosity and a lifelong appreciation for the natural world.

Happy exploring!
